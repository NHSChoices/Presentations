Performance information to support transparency and drive quality

By making our data available to all we aim to drive up quality and help create even better services.

Here you can see key data used by the NHS and local councils to monitor performance and shape the services you use. We’re continually adding new information, and working to make it as clear as possible, based on what people tell us they want to see.

Since the launch of My NHS in Autumn 2014, we’ve improved the site to make it easier to use. We’ve now changed the mental health data display so it can be sorted by trust. On hospitals, we’ve added independent sector hospitals, patient-reported outcomes, the Friends and Family Test, and a range of treatment data.

To keep improving, we need your feedback, so take a look, then tell us what you think of the site.

NHS England Medical Director's view

In the NHS and across the public sector we are committed to making sure information about the quality of our services is available and accessible to all. Making information readily available in one place allows people to hold us to account, not just for the amount of money we spend providing care but for how it is spent and what it delivers for the public. We know that there is already lots of data published on a range of websites but it can be difficult to find and harder to compare, even if you are an expert. On this site we have one place where organisations, professionals and the public can compare the performance of services across health and care, over a range of measures, and on local and national levels.

Overall, what this wealth of published data suggests is an encouraging picture. Despite having received little increase in funding over recent years, the NHS continues to provide high-quality care, often to the highest standards in the world. We are meeting the challenge of increasing demand and treating more people than ever before. Despite the growing numbers of frail older people with complex medical conditions, the number of avoidable emergency admissions has risen only slightly and infection rates continue to fall. For people with long-term conditions, emergency admissions to hospital have actually fallen. With some inexcusable exceptions, patient care is excellent and the majority of patients tell us that their experience of NHS care is positive. Perhaps, most importantly, the success of the NHS can be judged by the fact that, in part as a consequence of healthcare they receive, people are living longer than ever before.

Sir Bruce Keogh, NHS England Medical Director

Health outcomes

The effects of better care on life expectancy

In 2012, the number of potential years of men's life lost by causes that can be dealt with by better healthcare was 3,100 per 100,000 population. This is a fall of 31% since 2003.

Similarly in 2012, the number of potential years of women's life lost by causes that can be dealt with by better healthcare was 2,500 per 100,000 population. This is a fall of 27% since 2003.

The graph shows potential years of adult life lost by causes that can be dealt with by better healthcare.

Emergency hospital admissions for long-term conditions

Some long-term health conditions (such as asthma or epilepsy) can be prevented from getting suddenly worse (such as an asthma attack or epileptic fit) by better healthcare such as vaccination and changes to lifestyle. In 2012/13, there were 2,000 emergency admissions to hospital for people with such conditions. This was an increase of 4.3% from 2009/10.

This increase in these emergency admissions is most likely to be due to there now being more older people living with several different long-term conditions who experience sudden episodes of unrelated conditions such as urinary tract infections.

Superbugs (hospital acquired infections)

Between 2009/10 and 2013/14, the number of cases of MRSA bacteraemia decreased by 54.6%.

Between 2009/10 and 2013/14, the number of cases of C. difficile decreased by 47.8%.

'Patient experience'

Patients say their hospital care is improving. In 2013/14, the average patient experience score for hospital care was 77 out of 100. This was an increase of 1.3 points since 2009/10.

Data providers

Health and care data on MyNHS is compiled by the Department of Health and NHS England in collaboration with Public Health England and the Care Quality Commission.

The indicators selected for inclusion by these organisations draw on data that has been previously validated and published.

Consultant outcome data has been compiled and published by the relevant specialty associations.
